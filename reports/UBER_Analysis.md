## SECTION 1 — BUSINESS: Do I truly understand this business?

### 1. What does this company actually do? (Explain like to a 10-year-old)

Uber runs an app that connects three groups of people who have something to give with three groups who need it:

- **Mobility:** People who want a ride ↔ people with a car who'll drive them.
- **Delivery:** People who want food/groceries ↔ restaurants/stores ↔ couriers.
- **Freight:** Companies that need to ship stuff ↔ truckers with empty trailers.

Uber doesn't own the cars, the kitchens, or the trucks. It owns the matching engine — the maps, the pricing, the ratings, the fraud checks — and takes a small slice of every transaction (~25–30¢ of every dollar that flows through the app).

### 2. How do they make money, and from whom?

| Segment | What is sold | Who pays | Approx % of FY25 revenue | Take rate (Rev/Bookings) | Segment Adj EBITDA margin (% of bookings) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Mobility** | Rides | Riders | ~57% | ~30% | ~7–8% |
| **Delivery** | Food/grocery orders + Uber Direct + ads | Eaters / merchants | ~37% | ~19–20% | ~3.5–4% |
| **Freight** | Shipping bookings | Shippers | ~6% | ~100% (gross) | ~0% (break-even) |

A growing **fourth revenue source** is **advertising** — restaurants and CPGs pay to be promoted inside the app. This is now a ~$1.5B+ annualized run-rate business at very high incremental margins, and it is the single most under-appreciated lever in the P&L.

**Customers are mostly consumers, but the high-margin slice is increasingly merchants and advertisers.**

Reported margins (FY 2025):
- Gross margin: **39.7%** (revenue is largely net of driver/courier earnings in many markets; in others it's gross, hence the "lower" gross margin masks the platform's economics)
- Operating margin: **10.7%** (GAAP)
- FCF margin: **18.8%** — the more honest profitability lens for an asset-light platform.

### 3. Am I pretending to understand this business, or do I really?

**Honest answer: ~80%.** The parts I genuinely understand:
- Two-sided marketplaces with network effects.
- Why Mobility is a *better* business than Delivery (denser network, fewer SKUs, less price-sensitive).
- Why a platform that doesn't own assets can compound free cash flow rapidly once it crosses scale (FCF went $0.4B → $3.4B → $6.9B → $9.8B in 4 years).

What I am **not** sure I fully understand:
- **The accounting geography of "Gross Bookings vs. Revenue."** Uber re-classes revenue recognition (gross vs. net) by jurisdiction, by segment, and even by promotional structure. This is why headline gross margin looks like a logistics company even though the underlying take economics resemble a software marketplace. Anyone who tells you they can model this precisely from the outside is being clever, not honest.
- **The true unit economics in an autonomous-vehicle world.** Today Uber earns ~$2–3 of profit per trip after paying the driver. In an AV world, the driver line item disappears but so does Uber's exclusivity over supply. Whether Uber's take rate goes up, down, or sideways is a genuine unknown.
- **Insurance accruals.** Uber's "Cost of revenue" includes a large self-insurance reserve that is actuarial and lumpy. A bad year of California verdicts can swing margins 200bps.

So: I understand the business *directionally*. I do not understand it *precisely*. Honest position.

### 4. Would I be happy owning this for 10 years if the market shut tomorrow?

Cautious yes, with conditions.

**Reasons yes:**
- The matching-engine moat strengthens with scale (more drivers → shorter ETAs → more riders → more drivers). Uber has roughly **2–3x** Lyft's share in the US and is #1 or #2 in most of its 70+ countries.
- The business now generates **$10B+ of free cash flow** and is converting >95% of operating cash flow to FCF (capex is ~$330M against a $54B revenue base — almost surreal).
- A 10-year horizon is exactly the timeframe where the AV transition resolves itself in one direction or the other. Uber is positioned on *both* sides: partner to Waymo, Wayve, Aurora, WeRide, Pony.ai, etc., *and* operator of the largest demand network on earth. Owning the demand side has historically been a better business than owning the asset side.

**Conditions:**
- Management does not blow $10B of FCF on a "we need to own AVs too" pivot. (They tried this once with ATG and wisely sold it to Aurora in 2020.)
- Driver-classification regulation doesn't get globally codified into employment in a way that destroys the asset-light model.
- Buybacks continue at a pace that meaningfully shrinks share count (currently shrinking ~1.5–2% per year — fine but not aggressive given the FCF available).

### 5. What could kill this business?

In rough order of probability × severity:

| Risk | Probability (10y) | Severity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autonomous vehicles bypass Uber's demand layer** (e.g., Tesla, Waymo go consumer-direct at scale) | Medium | Severe | The single biggest existential question. Waymo One in SF already takes share. Mitigant: switching cost of consumer apps + Uber's data on demand patterns. |
| **Driver reclassification as employees** (US PRO Act, EU Platform Work Directive enforcement) | Medium-High | High | Would compress mobility EBITDA materially. Mitigant: model has survived AB5 era and the company has shown it can re-price. |
| **Insurance cost spiral** | Medium | Medium | Already a slow-burn issue in CA, NJ. Uber's now experimenting with consumer-paid surcharges. |
| **Recession crushes Delivery** | Medium | Medium | Delivery is more discretionary than mobility. But it's now profitable, not a cash sink. |
| **Major safety / data incident** | Low-Medium | High | 2016 breach is still in regulatory memory. |
| **Founder-tier governance accident** (Travis-style) | Low | High | Dara Khosrowshahi tenure has been clean — but board concentration of power matters. |

What would **not** kill it: a Lyft price war, a city ban (they've come back from London twice), a recession in any single market.

## SECTION 2 — MANAGEMENT: Can I trust these people with my capital?

### 1. Would I trust these people to run my family's money?

**Dara Khosrowshahi (CEO since Aug 2017).** Inherited a dumpster fire from Travis Kalanick. Eight years later: GAAP profitable, S&P 500 inclusion (Dec 2023), $10B FCF, no major scandals. Came from Expedia where he was a respected, low-drama operator (2005–2017).

**Prashanth Mahendra-Rajah (CFO since 2024)** replaced Nelson Chai. Came from Analog Devices — a real, disciplined operator. The shift from a finance-engineering CFO to a manufacturing-discipline CFO tracks Uber's evolution from "growth-at-all-costs" to "compound cash."

**Verdict:** Yes, with eyes open. This is not a founder-led mission company; it's a turnaround that has worked. The question is whether the second act (capital allocation in a maturing business) is as well-executed as the first (operational fix). Too early to know.

### 2. How do they treat minority shareholders when no one is watching?

**Mostly well, but with caveats:**

- **Pro-minority signals:**
  - Initiated buybacks in 2024 ($1.25B) and scaled aggressively in 2025 ($6.5B) — a real return of capital, not just SBC offset.
  - Killed the dual-class supervote structure inherited from Kalanick era in 2023 — one share, one vote.
  - Disclosed segment economics in plain English starting 2022 (Mobility/Delivery/Freight segment EBITDA was previously buried).
- **Yellow flags:**
  - **Stock-based compensation: $1.86B TTM (~3.5% of revenue, ~19% of FCF).** This is high. Buybacks at $6.5B are only ~$4.6B net of SBC — the real shareholder yield is closer to 3%, not 5%.
  - The Foodpanda Taiwan deal (~$950M, announced 2024, blocked by Taiwan regulators Dec 2024) showed they still have an M&A itch they need to manage.
  - Insider ownership is only **0.17%** — board and management own very little.

### 3. What do their last 10 years of capital allocation tell me?

| Era | Capital allocation | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| 2014–2018 (Kalanick) | Burned ~$10B on China, Southeast Asia, ATG (self-driving) before retreating from each | Disaster, but instructive |
| 2018–2020 (Dara, Phase 1) | Sold China to Didi (got equity), SE Asia to Grab (got equity), Russia to Yandex, ATG to Aurora (got equity). Bought Postmates ($2.65B) and Cornershop. | **Excellent** — converted losing businesses into stakes in winners. The Didi, Grab, and Aurora stakes are now real assets on the balance sheet (~$8B in long-term investments). |
| 2021–2023 (Dara, Phase 2) | Cost discipline. May 2022 "sea change" memo to staff: "We have to make sure our unit economics work… meritocracy at promotion." Stopped speculative M&A. | **Strong** |
| 2024–2026 (Dara, Phase 3) | Initiated buybacks, paid down some convertibles, attempted Foodpanda Taiwan (blocked), letter focus shifted to "Gross Bookings → FCF compounding" | **Good so far, watch closely** |

**10-year scorecard: B+.** They turned a $10B-burn business into a $10B-FCF business. They didn't do anything insane after the turnaround. They're not yet a master capital allocator (no buyback at peak fear, no dividend, no transformative acquisition), but they haven't destroyed value either.

### 4. Do they tell the truth in good years and in bad?

Reviewing three recent shareholder communications:

- **FY 2022 letter (a bad year — $9.1B GAAP loss from equity write-downs):** Dara owned it. "We made tough decisions on hiring… equity markdowns reflect a different macro." Did not hide behind "non-GAAP." Discussed unit economics in detail.
- **FY 2023 letter (the breakthrough year — first GAAP profit, S&P 500):** Did *not* spike the football. Explicitly warned that 2023's GAAP NI was flattered by equity revaluation gains and would not repeat.
- **FY 2024 letter:** Disclosed that $5.8B of net income came from a deferred tax valuation allowance release — a one-time non-cash benefit. They could have buried this. They led with it.

**Verdict: Above average for a US large-cap.** They consistently distinguish between "real" earnings (FCF, segment Adj. EBITDA) and "accounting" earnings (GAAP NI distorted by tax assets and equity stake mark-to-market). That is a tell of honest management.

The "sea change" memo (leaked May 2022) is also worth reading — Dara wrote it for internal consumption but knew it would leak, and it was unusually blunt about a coming culture shift. That kind of communication is rare.

### 5. Do they have real skin in the game, and for how long?

**This is the weakest area of the management thesis.**

- Total insider ownership: **0.17%** (~$258M at current price across all officers/directors).
- Dara Khosrowshahi: ~1.5M shares + options, roughly $110–150M position. Significant *for him* but he sells programmatically via 10b5-1 plans every quarter.
- Travis Kalanick (founder) sold his entire ~8.6% stake in 2019–2020.
- No founder remains on the cap table.
- Board members have token ownership.

**Compare to:** Meta (Zuckerberg ~13%), Amazon (Bezos ~9%), Berkshire (Buffett ~14%). Uber is firmly in the "professionally managed, non-founder-anchored" camp.

This is fine, but it means **incentives flow through comp plan design, not through long-term equity ownership.** The 2026 proxy is worth reading carefully on this — TSR-based PSUs would be a positive sign; pure RSU grants would be neutral-negative.

## SECTION 3 — PRICE: Am I paying a sensible price?

### 1. What am I paying today?

At $74.69:

| Lens | Multiple | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Trailing P/E | 18.5x | Distorted upward by tax assets — not the right lens |
| **Forward P/E (consensus 2026: $3.44)** | **21.7x** | Reset year — consensus EPS *down* YoY because 2025 had ~$4B tax benefit |
| **2027 consensus P/E ($4.43)** | **16.9x** | More representative |
| EV / EBITDA (TTM) | 22.0x | Optically high |
| EV / FCF (TTM) | **15.8x** | The right lens for an asset-light platform |
| **FCF yield** | **6.45%** | Plus ~14–16% expected FCF growth = mid-teens forward return potential |
| PEG | 0.64 | Cheap if growth holds |
| Price / Tangible Book | 10.2x | Asset-light; not meaningful |
| ROIC | 19.3% (well above 10% WACC) | Real economic profit creation |

**Most honest single number: 16x EV/FCF on a business compounding FCF ~25% over the past 4 years.** That is not expensive. It is not the bargain it was at $40 in 2022, but it is reasonable.

### 2. What is the market implicitly expecting, and do I agree?

Reverse-DCF check at $74.69 (back-of-envelope, 10% discount rate, 3% terminal):
- Current FCF: $9.8B
- Required FCF growth for current price to make sense: **~10–12% CAGR for 10 years**, then 3% terminal.

**What I think will actually happen** (base case):
- Gross bookings: 15% CAGR for 3 years, decelerating to 10% by year 5.
- Take rate: stable to slightly up (advertising mix shift offsets AV partner economics).
- FCF margin: 18% → 22% as Delivery scales and advertising mixes up.
- **FCF growth: ~15–18% near-term, 10–12% mid-term.**

**The market is pricing in roughly what I'd model.** No big upside-surprise embedded; no big disaster embedded either. This is a "fair price for a good business" situation, not a "great price for a great business."

The 52-week decline of -18.6% reflects two specific worries: (a) Tesla robotaxi launch in Austin and (b) Waymo's SF/LA expansion. Both real, but in my view over-discounted in the short term — Waymo today does ~250k rides/week vs Uber's ~33M rides/day globally. The math doesn't yet support panic.

### 3. What would I pay if I were buying the whole business?

If I were buying 100% of Uber privately at a 10% required return:

- Normalized FCF (mid-cycle, post-tax-benefit noise): **~$11B**
- Required return: 10%
- Terminal growth: 3% (real-world, not financier math)
- **Implied EV: $11B / (10% − 3%) = ~$157B**
- Less net debt ~$6B = **Equity value ~$151B = ~$74/share**

**Conclusion: today's price is approximately what a rational private buyer would pay.** It is not a steal. It is fair.

I'd start nibbling under **$65** (a ~13% margin of safety to my private-market value, or ~13x EV/FCF). I'd back up the truck below **$55** (-25%, where you'd be buying at ~11x EV/FCF for a 15%-growing platform — a genuinely good deal).

### 4. At what price would I sell, and why? (Decided before I buy, not after)

Three sell triggers, decided in advance:

1. **Price-based:** Above **$120** (~25x forward EV/FCF, ~27x 2027 P/E). At that level the market is pricing in flawless AV transition + 20%+ FCF growth forever. I would trim 50% of position.
2. **Fundamental:** If FCF margin contracts for 2 consecutive quarters with no clear one-off cause — that would suggest take rate compression from AV partners is real and structural. Exit fully.
3. **Capital allocation:** If management announces a >$10B acquisition or restarts large speculative M&A (re-entering China, building own AV fleet, etc.) — exit fully. The thesis depends on them *not* swinging for fences.

### 5. If this drops 40% next month, do I buy more or panic?

A 40% drop puts the stock at **~$45** (market cap ~$92B, EV/FCF ~9x).

**The honest pre-commitment: I buy more, aggressively, *unless* the drop is accompanied by one of three things:**

1. **Driver reclassification ruling** federally in the US or in a top-3 market (Brazil, UK, Mexico). This changes the unit economics structurally.
2. **A confirmed step-function in AV consumer adoption** — e.g., Tesla operating 100k+ public robotaxis or Waymo signing a Tesla-style deal with another major OEM, *and* announcing direct consumer apps in 10+ cities.
3. **Management departure or governance event** (Dara leaves, large insider sale, surprise CFO change).

Absent those, a 40% drop on macro/sentiment is the buy of a 3-year window. The business at 9x FCF generating $10B+ would be silly cheap.

**The pre-commitment matters more than the analysis.** Most investors fail not because they pick wrong, but because they sell at $45 and buy back at $90.

This is a good business at a fair price, not a great business at a great price. For a portfolio, I'd size it as a 2–3% position today, with dry powder reserved to scale to 5–6% if the AV-fear sell-off deepens.
